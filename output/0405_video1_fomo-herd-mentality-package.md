## 2. VIDEO SCRIPT

**The Psychology of FOMO and Herd Mentality in Investing**

[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]

Have you ever bought something — a stock, a crypto token, a property — not because you understood its value, but because everyone around you seemed to be making money and you couldn't stand watching from the sidelines?

People think financial decisions are rational. They think smart people don't fall for hype. But psychology tells a completely different story. [PAUSE]

Today we're breaking down why your brain is wired to follow the crowd — and how the fear of missing out silently hijacks your financial decisions.

[INTRO — 0:30–1:00]

By the end of this video, you'll understand three things: why FOMO feels like a survival instinct, how herd mentality creates financial bubbles, and what psychological tools you can use to think independently when everyone else is panicking or chasing.

Let's break down what's really going on inside your mind.

[BODY — 1:00–end]

**One: Your brain treats social exclusion like physical pain.**

Evolutionarily, being separated from the group meant death. Your brain still operates on that logic. When you see everyone profiting from something and you're not involved, your brain's anterior cingulate cortex — the same region that processes physical pain — lights up. This is called *social pain overlap theory*. Your brain literally hurts when you feel left out. So you buy in, not to make money, but to stop the pain of exclusion.

**Two: You confuse excitement with evidence.**

When an asset is rising fast, your brain releases dopamine — the same chemical triggered by food, social validation, and risk-taking. Psychologists call this *affect heuristic*. You start equating the positive emotion you feel with the logical conclusion that the investment must be good. The higher the hype, the more your brain says "this is definitely real" — even when the data says otherwise. [PAUSE]

**Three: You instinctively trust numbers over logic.**

When dozens of people around you are buying the same thing, your brain uses *social proof* — a cognitive shortcut coined by Robert Cialdini. Instead of analyzing the asset yourself, you default to the assumption: if this many people believe in it, they must know something I don't. The problem? In a bubble, everyone is making the exact same logical error. The crowd isn't wise — it's just loud.

**Four: You become overconfident after a single win.**

If you made money once by following a trend, your brain stores that as proof that you're good at investing. This is called *self-attribution bias* — you credit your skill for the win but blame external factors for any loss. This makes you double down on the next trend with even more confidence, completely ignoring that your first success might have been pure luck. [PAUSE]

**Five: You panic-sell not because of logic, but because of emotional contagion.**

When the market drops, you don't calmly assess your portfolio. Your brain's amygdala activates — the fight-or-flight center. And emotions are contagious. Psychologists call this *emotional contagion* — you absorb the fear of people around you, even through a screen. This is why crashes are always sharper and faster than rallies. Fear spreads like a virus.

**Six: You fall for the "this time is different" illusion.**

Every bubble in history — tulip mania, the dot-com crash, the 2008 housing crisis, crypto surges — was built on the same belief: the old rules no longer apply. Psychologists refer to this as *normalcy bias* combined with *optimism bias*. You assume that because things have been going up, they'll keep going up. And you dismiss anyone who warns you as simply not understanding the opportunity. [PAUSE]

**Seven: Independent thinking is physically uncomfortable.**

Going against the crowd triggers what psychologists call *cognitive dissonance*. Your brain has to hold two conflicting beliefs at the same time: "everyone says buy" and "the numbers say don't." This mental tension is genuinely uncomfortable — so most people resolve it by just following the crowd. It's easier. It hurts less. But it's exactly what costs them money.

[OUTRO — last 60 seconds]

Let's recap. FOMO isn't greed — it's your brain's ancient survival system misfiring in a modern financial world. Herd mentality isn't stupidity — it's a cognitive shortcut that worked for our ancestors but fails us in markets. And independent thinking isn't about being contrarian — it's about pausing long enough to notice when your emotions are making your decisions for you.
